Other Pronouns

Beyond the personal pronouns and the article, Koine Greek employs a variety of pronouns that serve crucial roles in conveying emphasis, reference, indefiniteness, reflexivity, reciprocity, and interrogation. These include demonstrative, reflexive, reciprocal, indefinite, and interrogative pronouns. Each group follows distinct morphological patterns across gender, number, and case, and their syntactic usage interacts significantly with the article and broader discourse context.

Key Paradigms

Demonstrative Pronouns – οὗτος (“this”), ἐκεῖνος (“that”)

Demonstratives function adjectivally and substantivally, pointing out specific entities in space, time, or discourse. They decline similarly to first and second declension adjectives, but with irregular stems.

Case Masculine (οὗτος) Feminine (αὕτη) Neuter (τοῦτο)
Nominative Sg. οὗτος αὕτη τοῦτο
Genitive Sg. τούτου ταύτης τούτου
Dative Sg. τούτῳ ταύτῃ τούτῳ
Accusative Sg. τοῦτον ταύτην τοῦτο
Nominative Pl. οὗτοι αὗται ταῦτα
Genitive Pl. τούτων τούτων τούτων
Dative Pl. τούτοις ταύταις τούτοις
Accusative Pl. τούτους ταύτας ταῦτα

The demonstrative ἐκεῖνος (“that”) follows the same endings as a regular 2-1-2 adjective, declining straightforwardly with the stem ἐκειν-.

Reflexive Pronouns – ἐμαυτοῦ, σεαυτοῦ, ἑαυτοῦ

Reflexive pronouns refer back to the subject and do not appear in the nominative case. They are compounded from a personal pronoun and the genitive form of the adjective αὐτός. These pronouns are used for the first, second, and third persons respectively.

Case 1st Person (ἐμαυτοῦ) 2nd Person (σεαυτοῦ) 3rd Person (ἑαυτοῦ)
Genitive Sg. ἐμαυτοῦ σεαυτοῦ ἑαυτοῦ
Dative Sg. ἐμαυτῷ σεαυτῷ ἑαυτῷ
Accusative Sg. ἐμαυτόν σεαυτόν ἑαυτόν

The reflexive for the third person, ἑαυτοῦ, is frequently used for all genders and numbers when the reflexive sense is required for the subject of the clause.

Reciprocal Pronoun – ἀλλήλων (“one another”)

This pronoun appears only in the plural and typically in the genitive case, reflecting mutual or reciprocal action. It behaves like a second declension adjective in form, although only a subset of case forms is commonly attested.

Case Masculine/Feminine
Genitive Pl. ἀλλήλων
Dative Pl. ἀλλήλοις / ἀλλήλαις
Accusative Pl. ἀλλήλους / ἀλλήλας

Indefinite Pronoun – τις, τι (“someone, something”)

The indefinite pronoun denotes an unspecified person or thing. It uses enclitic accentuation and resembles the interrogative in form but is distinguished by stress: τις (indefinite) vs. τίς (interrogative). It follows a third declension pattern.

Case Masculine/Feminine Neuter
Nominative Sg. τις τι
Genitive Sg. τινός τινός
Dative Sg. τινί τινί
Accusative Sg. τινά τι

Interrogative Pronoun – τίς, τί (“who?, what?”)

The interrogative pronoun poses direct or indirect questions. It is identical in form to the indefinite except for its accent (acute on the first syllable). Its declension mirrors that of τις.

Case Masculine/Feminine Neuter
Nominative Sg. τίς τί
Genitive Sg. τίνος τίνος
Dative Sg. τίνι τίνι
Accusative Sg. τίνα τί

Usage and Syntax

These pronouns vary morphologically across gender and number, though some (such as τις and τίς) maintain neuter distinctions only in the nominative and accusative singular. Demonstratives frequently combine with the article to mark emphasis or near vs. far deixis. Reflexives require a subject antecedent and must align in person and number. The reciprocal ἀλλήλων implies mutuality and never appears in the singular. Indefinite and interrogative forms differ only in accentuation but require careful contextual reading to distinguish between them.

In discourse, demonstratives frequently establish or maintain referential cohesion, while reflexives and reciprocals express intra-clausal relationships. The interaction between pronouns and the article is especially prominent with demonstratives and interrogatives, where their use can imply definiteness or emphasis, depending on word order and clause structure.
