Prepositions

Prepositions in New Testament Greek are function words that show the relationship between nouns (or pronouns) and other elements in the sentence. The meaning of a preposition is often governed by the case of the noun it governs, and many prepositions change meaning depending on whether their object is in the genitive, dative, or accusative case. Prepositions are frequently used with verbs, especially participles, to clarify spatial, temporal, or metaphorical relationships.

Key Paradigms

Prepositions That Take One Case

Preposition Case Meaning
ἐν Dative in, on, among
σύν Dative with
ἀπό Genitive from, away from
ἐκ (ἐξ) Genitive out of, from
εἰς Accusative into, to, toward
ἀντί Genitive instead of, in place of

Prepositions That Take Two Cases

Preposition Case Meaning
διά Genitive through (means, agency)
διά Accusative because of, on account of
μετά Genitive with (association)
μετά Accusative after (time or sequence)
ὑπέρ Genitive for, on behalf of
ὑπέρ Accusative above, beyond
ὑπό Genitive by (agent, cause)
ὑπό Accusative under (motion or position)
περί Genitive concerning, about
περί Accusative around, about (spatial)
κατά Genitive down from, against
κατά Accusative according to, throughout
πρός Accusative to, toward, with (direction or goal)
πρός Dative at, near (rare; poetic or LXX-style)

Prepositions That Take Three Cases

Preposition Case Meaning
ἐπί Genitive on, upon, over (position or authority)
ἐπί Dative on, at, near (rest or location)
ἐπί Accusative onto, over, against (motion or direction)
παρά Genitive from (a person or source)
παρά Dative with, beside (location or proximity)
παρά Accusative alongside, past, beside (motion)

Conceptual Categories

  • Meaning Variation with Case: Many Greek prepositions shift meaning depending on the case of their object. For example, διά + genitive means "through," whereas διά + accusative means "because of." These distinctions are essential for accurate translation and interpretation.
  • Syntax with Verbs and Participles: Prepositions often govern participial phrases or complement finite verbs. Their objects may be nouns or pronouns functioning as adverbial modifiers, specifying direction, location, manner, or cause.
  • Spatial, Temporal, and Metaphorical Meanings: Prepositions can indicate:
    • Spatial: εἰς τὸν οἶκον ("into the house"), ἐν τῷ κόσμῳ ("in the world")
    • Temporal: μετὰ ταῦτα ("after these things"), ἐπὶ τῇ ἡμέρᾳ ("on the day")
    • Metaphorical: διὰ πίστεως ("through faith"), ὑπὸ νόμου ("under law")
    A single preposition can function in more than one domain depending on context.
