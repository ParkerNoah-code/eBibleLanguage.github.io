1. Conceptual Summary

Third declension nouns in Koine Greek exhibit the most morphologically diverse noun patterns. They are characterized by consonantal or irregular stems, and unlike the first and second declensions, the nominative singular is often unpredictable. Parsing third declension nouns requires special attention to the genitive singular, which reveals the true stem. These nouns decline according to case and number across the same five-case system: nominative, genitive, dative, accusative, and vocative. Definite articles follow the regular declension patterns but are critical in disambiguating gender and case.

3. Parsing Features

  • Stems: Often derived from genitive singular by removing -ος (e.g., πατρ-, σαρκ-, σωματ-).
  • Key Endings:
    • Gen. Sg.: -ος (shows stem)
    • Dat. Sg.: -ι (added to stem, often with phonological changes)
    • Acc. Sg.: -α / -ν, depending on gender and phonology
    • Nom./Acc. Neuter Pl.: -α
    • Dat. Pl.: -σι(ν)
  • Neuter Rule: Nom. = Acc. in all numbers
  • Gen. Pl. Uniform: -ων, same as other declensions
  • Definite article agreement remains a primary indicator of case/gender
  • Phonological shifts: Nominative forms often suppress final consonants (e.g., σάρξ from σαρκ-)
  • Common irregularities: vowel gradation, assimilation, dropped consonants

4. “Form Spotting” Tips

  • Always check the genitive singular to determine the true stem—nominative forms are deceptive.
  • Watch for typical consonant clusters in dat. pl. (-σιν): these often indicate 3rd declension dative plural forms.
  • Neuter nouns follow strict rules: Nom. = Acc.; Pl. always ends in -α.
  • Look for endings like -ος (Gen. Sg.), -ι (Dat. Sg.), and -σι(ν) (Dat. Pl.) as reliable third declension indicators.
  • Internal stem changes (ablaut, consonant loss) are common—compare σῶμα (nom.) vs. σώματος (gen.).

5. Verse Examples

John 1:14 — καὶ ὁ λόγος σὰρξ ἐγένετο
“And the Word became flesh.”
σὰρξ (Nom. Sg. Fem., 3rd decl., stem σαρκ-) – subject complement.

Romans 12:1 — παραστῆσαι τὰ σώματα ὑμῶν θυσίαν ζῶσαν
“to present your bodies as a living sacrifice”
τὰ σώματα (Acc. Pl. Neut., 3rd decl., stem σωματ-) – direct object.

Hebrews 12:9 — τοὺς μὲν τῆς σαρκὸς πατέρας
“the fathers of our flesh”
τῆς σαρκὸς (Gen. Sg. Fem., 3rd decl., stem σαρκ-) – possession.

6. Third Declension Subtypes

Third declension nouns fall into identifiable morphological subtypes. These distinctions affect predictable case endings and help in parsing irregular forms.

i-Stem Nouns

  • Common in feminine nouns like πόλις and πίστις
  • Characterized by an iota element in the stem, often visible in the genitive plural and dative plural
Case πόλις (fem.) Plural
Nominative ἡ πόλις αἱ πόλεις
Genitive τῆς πόλεως τῶν πόλεων
Dative τῇ πόλει ταῖς πόλεσι(ν)
Accusative τὴν πόλιν τὰς πόλεις

Consonant-Stem Nouns

  • Include nouns like σάρξ (stem: σαρκ-) and πατήρ (stem: πατρ-)
  • May experience phonological reduction in nominative singular (e.g., consonant loss)

Mixed-Stem Nouns

  • Appear irregular due to blending of consonant and i-stem features
  • Example: βασιλεύς (king), which patterns uniquely in plural
Case βασιλεύς (masc.) Plural
Nominative ὁ βασιλεύς οἱ βασιλεῖς
Genitive τοῦ βασιλέως τῶν βασιλέων
Dative τῷ βασιλεῖ τοῖς βασιλεῦσι(ν)
Accusative τὸν βασιλέα τοὺς βασιλεῖς
