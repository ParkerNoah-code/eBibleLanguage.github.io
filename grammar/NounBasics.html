1. Conceptual Summary

In Koine Greek, nouns decline to express case and number. There are three major declension patterns, of which two are discussed here. Each noun appears in one of five cases: nominative (subject), genitive (possession or source), dative (indirect object or means), accusative (direct object or extent), and vocative (direct address). Declension endings also indicate whether a noun is singular or plural. Recognizing these inflectional patterns is crucial for identifying the grammatical function of words in Greek sentences, where word order is fluid.

3. Parsing Features

  • Stems: η/α for 1st decl.; ο/ου for 2nd decl.
  • Key Endings:
    • Gen. Sg.: -ης / -ας / -ου
    • Dat. Sg.: -ῃ / -ᾳ / -ῳ
    • Acc. Sg.: -ην / -αν / -ον
    • Nom./Acc. Neuter Pl.: -α
  • Neuter rule: Nom. = Acc. in all numbers
  • Gen. Pl. uniform: -ων for all genders/declensions
  • Definite article agreement helps disambiguate case/gender
  • Mixed types: e.g., δόξα follows α-type in Nom./Acc., η-type elsewhere

4. “Form Spotting” Tips

  • η-type and α-type differ in Gen./Dat. Singular—watch for these contrasts
  • Articles provide immediate clues to case and gender—learn them cold
  • Neuter forms: Nom. = Acc. always; plural ends in -α
  • Gen. Plural: always ends in -ων—scan for it to simplify parsing
  • Look for consonant-ending stems (e.g., δόξα) that cause irregularities

5. Verse Examples

Matthew 5:14 — ὑμεῖς ἐστὲ τὸ φῶς τοῦ κόσμου
“Y'all are the light of the world.”
τὸ φῶς (Nom. Sg. Neuter, 2nd decl.) – subject complement; τοῦ κόσμου (Gen. Sg. Masc., 2nd decl.) – possession (“of the world”).

Mark 4:14 — ὁ σπείρων τὸν λόγον σπείρει
“The sower sows the word.”
ὁ σπείρων (Nom. Sg. Masc., participle) – subject; τὸν λόγον (Acc. Sg. Masc., 2nd decl.) – direct object (“the word”).
