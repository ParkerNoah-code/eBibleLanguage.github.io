Optative Verbs

The optative mood in New Testament Greek is the mood of possibility, wish, and indirect discourse. Though rare in the New Testament, it appears in quotations, indirect speech, or stylized narrative. Its forms are recognizable by distinctive endings and vowel-lengthening patterns. In meaning, the optative expresses what might happen or what one wishes would happen. It is also a common substitution for the subjunctive in indirect speech when the main verb is in a past tense. Parsing and recognition of the optative is essential for understanding the nuances of reported or potential action in Greek.

Key Paradigms

Present Active Optative – λύοιμι ("I might be loosing")

Person Singular Plural
1st λύοιμι λύοιμεν
2nd λύοις λύοιτε
3rd λύοι λύοιεν

Present Middle/Passive Optative – λυοίμην ("I might be being loosed")

Person Singular Plural
1st λυοίμην λυοίμεθα
2nd λύοιο λύοισθε
3rd λύοιτο λύοιντο

Aorist Active Optative – λῦσαιμι ("I might loose")

Person Singular Plural
1st λῦσαιμι λῦσαιμεν
2nd λῦσαις / λῦσειας λῦσαιτε
3rd λῦσαι / λῦσειε(ν) λῦσαιεν / λύσειαν

Aorist Middle Optative – λυσαίμην ("I might loose [for myself]")

Person Singular Plural
1st λυσαίμην λυσαίμεθα
2nd λύσαιο λύσαισθε
3rd λύσαιτο λύσαιντο

Aorist Passive Optative – λυθείην ("I might be loosed")

Person Singular Plural
1st λυθείην λυθείημεν / λυθεῖμεν
2nd λυθείης λυθείητε / λυθεῖτε
3rd λυθείη / λυθείη(ν) λυθείησαν / λυθεῖεν

Usage and Recognition

The optative mood frequently signals a wish or desire when used independently: for instance, εἴθε λύοιτο ("May he be loosed!"). More often in the New Testament, it is used in indirect discourse, typically following a verb of speaking or perception in the past tense. For example, in reported speech: ἔλεγεν ὅτι λύοιτο ("He was saying that he might be loosed").

Though not common in Koine Greek, the optative remains a grammatical marker of elegant or formal style, and familiarity with its forms is essential for parsing correctly in texts such as Luke-Acts or the Septuagint. Recognition of optative forms typically involves looking for the characteristic endings -ιμι, -ις, -ιεν or -ίην, -ίης (for passive), along with vowel lengthening or diphthong changes in the stem.
