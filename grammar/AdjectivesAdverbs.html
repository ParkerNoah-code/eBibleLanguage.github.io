Adjectives and Adverbs

In New Testament Greek, adjectives function to modify nouns and must agree in case, number, and gender with the nouns they modify. Adverbs, by contrast, modify verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs and are typically derived from adjectives. Understanding the morphology and syntactical usage of adjectives and adverbs is essential for accurate interpretation of Greek texts.

Key Paradigms

Nominative Singular καλός καλή καλόν
Genitive Singular καλοῦ καλῆς καλοῦ
Accusative Singular καλόν καλήν καλόν

Genitive Plural καλῶν καλῶν καλῶν

Adverb Formation – καλῶς, δικαίως

Many Greek adverbs are formed from adjectives, especially of the first and second declensions, by replacing the final vowel with -ως.

  • καλός → καλῶς ("well")
  • δίκαιος → δικαίως ("justly")

Conceptual Categories

  • Agreement: Adjectives must agree with the nouns they modify in case, number, and gender, though not necessarily in declension type. This morphological harmony is critical for proper syntactical interpretation.
  • Predicate vs. Attributive Position:
    • Attributive position: The adjective appears with the article and noun (e.g., ὁ καλὸς λόγος, "the good word").
    • Predicate position: The adjective appears outside or without the article (e.g., ὁ λόγος καλός, "the word is good").
  • Degrees of Comparison: Greek adjectives can express different degrees of intensity:
    • Positive: καλός ("good")
    • Comparative: βελτίων ("better")
    • Superlative: βέλτιστος ("best")
    Comparative and superlative forms are often irregular and must be learned individually.
  • Adverb Formation Rules: Most adverbs from 1st/2nd declension adjectives are formed by adding -ως to the stem. Some irregular adverbs exist, particularly those derived from 3rd declension adjectives or compounded roots.
