Numbers

Greek numbers appear in two primary forms: cardinal and ordinal. Cardinal numbers express quantity ("one," "two," "three"), while ordinal numbers express sequence ("first," "second," "third"). Unlike nouns or verbs, numbers in Greek often exhibit irregular forms and require memorization, especially in the lower range. They are used frequently in narrative, discourse, and apocalyptic contexts, making them essential for beginning students.

Cardinal Numbers

Cardinal numbers are used to count discrete items. Only the numbers from one to three, and sometimes four, agree with the nouns they modify in gender, case, and number. From five upward, the cardinal numbers tend to be indeclinable or irregularly declined and are typically treated as neuter plural or invariable forms.

Ordinal Numbers

Ordinal numbers describe order or position in a sequence. They behave like regular first- and second-declension adjectives and agree with the nouns they modify in gender, case, and number. While only the lower ordinals are frequently used in the New Testament, their regular declension makes them easier to integrate into sentence structure.

Numerical Usage in Context

In the New Testament, numbers function as adjectives and agree with the noun they modify when applicable. Cardinal numbers answer "how many?" and often appear with direct objects, subjects, or appositional constructions. Ordinal numbers answer "which in order?" and typically introduce lists or sequences (e.g., generations, days, trumpet blasts). Awareness of these forms enhances textual precision, especially in narrative chronology and apocalyptic enumeration.
