Imperfect Indicative Verbs

The imperfect indicative in New Testament Greek expresses past action that is continuous, repeated, or descriptive in nature. Unlike the aorist, which conveys punctiliar or undefined action, the imperfect presents actions in the past as ongoing or habitual. This form uses the present stem, includes an augment to mark past time, and employs secondary endings.

Key Paradigms

Imperfect Active Indicative – ἔλυον ("I was loosing")

Person Singular Plural
1st ἔλυον ἐλύομεν
2nd ἔλυες ἐλύετε
3rd ἔλυε(ν) ἔλυον

Imperfect Middle/Passive Indicative – ἐλυόμην ("I was being loosed")

Person Singular Plural
1st ἐλυόμην ἐλυόμεθα
2nd ἐλύου ἐλύεσθε
3rd ἐλύετο ἐλύοντο

Conceptual Categories

  • Secondary Endings: The imperfect indicative uses secondary personal endings to signal past tense. These endings are different from those used in the present system.
    • Active: -ον, -ες, -ε(ν), -ομεν, -ετε, -ον
    • Middle/Passive: -όμην, -ου, -ετο, -όμεθα, -εσθε, -οντο
  • Present Stem: The imperfect is formed from the present tense stem (e.g., λυ- from λύω), but unlike the present tense, it describes actions in past time.
  • Use of Augment: An augment (typically ε-) is prefixed to the verb to indicate past time reference. This is a key morphological marker of the imperfect and other past tenses.
  • Continuous Past Aspect: The imperfect expresses action in progress or repeated action in the past. It is often translated as "was doing" or "used to do." For example, ἔλυον may mean "I was loosing" or "I used to loose," depending on context.
