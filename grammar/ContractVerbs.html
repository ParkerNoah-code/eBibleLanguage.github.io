Contract Verbs

Contract verbs in New Testament Greek are verbs whose stems end in a short vowel—α, ε, or ο—that undergoes regular vowel contraction when followed by the thematic vowels of the present tense endings. These contractions result in forms that appear irregular to the untrained eye but follow consistent phonological patterns. The three types of contract verbs are referred to as α-contract, ε-contract, and ο-contract verbs.

Key Paradigms

α-Contract Verb – τιμάω ("I honor")

Person Singular Plural
1st τιμῶ τιμῶμεν
2nd τιμᾷς τιμᾶτε
3rd τιμᾷ τιμῶσι(ν)

ε-Contract Verb – ποιέω ("I do, make")

Person Singular Plural
1st ποιῶ ποιοῦμεν
2nd ποιεῖς ποιεῖτε
3rd ποιεῖ ποιοῦσι(ν)

ο-Contract Verb – δηλόω ("I reveal")

Person Singular Plural
1st δηλῶ δηλοῦμεν
2nd δηλοῖς δηλοῦτε
3rd δηλοῖ δηλοῦσι(ν)

Conceptual Categories

  • Contracting Rules: When the final vowel of the stem (α, ε, or ο) meets a following vowel from the present tense endings, contraction occurs. Each type follows predictable patterns:
    • α + ε/ο → α; α + ει → ᾷ; α + ω → ῶ
    • ε + ω → οῦ; ε + ει → εῖ; ε + ο → ου
    • ο + ε → ου; ο + ει → οῖ; ο + ω → οῦ
    These patterns are consistent and foundational for recognizing contract forms.
  • Accentuation Changes: Contraction often results in a long vowel or diphthong, which affects the placement of the accent. Generally, the accent recedes when the contraction lengthens the syllable, often leading to circumflex accents (e.g., τιμᾷ, ποιεῖ, δηλοῦσιν).
  • Middle/Passive Adjustments: Contract verbs in the middle/passive voice undergo similar contractions, though the reflexive/passive endings remain. For instance:
    • τιμῶμαι (from τιμάομαι), ποιοῦμαι (from ποιέομαι), δηλοῦται (from δηλόεται)
    As with the active forms, the contractions follow regular phonological rules, and correct parsing depends on recognizing these transformations.
