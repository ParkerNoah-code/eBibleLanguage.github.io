Infinitives

The infinitive in New Testament Greek is a verbal noun. It expresses verbal action in an abstract or non-finite manner—without regard to person or number—and often functions grammatically like a noun. Greek infinitives preserve distinctions of aspect and voice, and they are frequently employed in indirect discourse, with impersonal verbs, and after certain prepositions.

Key Paradigms

Present Active Infinitive – λύειν ("to be loosing")

Formed by adding -ειν to the present stem (λυ-), this infinitive expresses an imperfective aspect—ongoing, repeated, or habitual action without reference to its beginning or end. It is used when the focus is on the process rather than the result.

Aorist Active Infinitive – λῦσαι ("to loose")

Built from the aorist stem, typically marked by an augment-stripped form and the suffix -σαι. The aorist infinitive reflects a perfective aspect, portraying the action as a whole or as a complete event. It does not denote past time but rather undivided action.

Perfect Active Infinitive – λελυκέναι ("to have loosed")

Derived from the perfect stem (with the reduplication and κ-perfect marker), this form emphasizes the resulting state of a completed action. It conveys a stative aspect, describing an action whose effects or consequences persist.

Aorist Passive Infinitive – λυθῆναι ("to be loosed")

Formed using the first aorist passive stem and the ending -ῆναι. This infinitive expresses perfective aspect in the passive voice, viewing the action as a whole and completed from the standpoint of the subject receiving the action.

Conceptual Categories

Aspectual Distinctions

Unlike English infinitives, Greek infinitives encode verbal aspect clearly. The present infinitive (λύειν) focuses on the process or duration of the action. The aorist infinitive (λῦσαι) views the action as a unit or whole. The perfect infinitive (λελυκέναι) emphasizes the state resulting from a completed action. This aspectual precision plays a significant exegetical role, particularly in theological contexts where the nuance between process and completion affects interpretation.

Use in Indirect Discourse

Infinitives often appear in indirect statements after verbs of saying, thinking, knowing, or perceiving (e.g., λέγω, οἶδα, δοκέω). The subject of the infinitive is typically in the accusative case—a construction known as the accusative and infinitive. For example: λέγει τὸν ἄνθρωπον λῦσαι τὸν δοῦλον ("He says that the man loosed the slave").

Use with Prepositions and Impersonal Verbs

Several prepositions govern infinitives, often forming idiomatic expressions. For example, πρὸ τοῦ λῦσαι means "before loosing," while ἐν τῷ λύειν conveys "while loosing" or "during the act of loosing." Such constructions contribute to temporal or causal nuance.

Infinitives are also used with impersonal verbs and expressions, such as δεῖ ("it is necessary") or ἔξεστιν ("it is lawful"). In such cases, the infinitive functions as the true subject of the sentence: δεῖ λῦσαι τὸν δοῦλον ("It is necessary to loose the slave").
