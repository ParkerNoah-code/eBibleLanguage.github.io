Subjunctive Verbs

The subjunctive mood in New Testament Greek expresses potential, contingent, or desired action. Unlike the indicative, which presents actions as factual or actual, the subjunctive mood introduces uncertainty, intention, or expectation. It is commonly used in subordinate clauses and is marked by a distinctive morphological feature: the lengthening of the connecting vowel (ο/ε becomes ω/η), without the presence of an augment.

Key Paradigms

Present Active Subjunctive – λύω ("that I may loose")

Person Singular Plural
1st λύω λύωμεν
2nd λύῃς λύητε
3rd λύῃ λύωσι(ν)

Present Middle/Passive Subjunctive – λύωμαι ("that I may be loosed")

Person Singular Plural
1st λύωμαι λυώμεθα
2nd λύῃ λύησθε
3rd λύηται λύωνται

Aorist Active Subjunctive – λύσω ("that I may loose" – punctiliar aspect)

Person Singular Plural
1st λύσω λύσωμεν
2nd λύσῃς λύσητε
3rd λύσῃ λύσωσι(ν)

Aorist Middle Subjunctive – λύσωμαι ("that I may loose for myself")

Person Singular Plural
1st λύσωμαι λυσώμεθα
2nd λύσῃ λύσησθε
3rd λύσηται λύσωνται

Aorist Passive Subjunctive – λυθῶ ("that I may be loosed")

Person Singular Plural
1st λυθῶ λυθῶμεν
2nd λυθῇς λυθῆτε
3rd λυθῇ λυθῶσι(ν)

Conceptual Categories

Unlike the indicative mood, the subjunctive does not use an augment, even in aorist forms. Instead, its chief morphological marker is the lengthened connecting vowel: ω for first person singular and plural, ῃ, ῃς, and ηται in various second and third person forms. The subjunctive often appears in volitional contexts (expressing will or command) and in hypothetical situations (particularly in conditional sentences).

It is most frequently found in subordinate clauses introduced by particles such as ἵνα (“in order that”), ἐάν (“if”), ὅταν (“whenever”), or μή (“lest”), which signal purpose, conditionality, or prohibition. For instance, in the sentence ἵνα ζωῇς, the subjunctive verb ζῇς indicates the desired result or purpose of the preceding clause.

While the present subjunctive emphasizes continuous or repeated action in a potential or future framework, the aorist subjunctive presents the action as undefined or punctiliar, focusing on the action as a whole rather than its ongoing nature. This distinction is aspectual, not temporal, and understanding it is essential for accurate exegesis of New Testament syntax.
