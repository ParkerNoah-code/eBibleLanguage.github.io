Aorist Indicative Verbs

The aorist indicative in New Testament Greek is the primary tense used to express simple, undefined action, often within narrative contexts. It does not describe how an action unfolds but simply that it occurred. The aorist is not inherently past in meaning, but in the indicative mood, it typically refers to past events. It utilizes an augment at the beginning and distinct tense-forming suffixes (e.g., -σα- or modified stems) depending on whether the verb is first or second aorist.

Key Paradigms

First Aorist Active Indicative – ἔλυσα ("I loosed")

Person Singular Plural
1st ἔλυσα ἐλύσαμεν
2nd ἔλυσας ἐλύσατε
3rd ἔλυσε(ν) ἔλυσαν

Second Aorist Active Indicative – ἔλαβον ("I received")

Person Singular Plural
1st ἔλαβον ἐλάβομεν
2nd ἔλαβες ἐλάβετε
3rd ἔλαβε(ν) ἔλαβον

Aorist Middle Indicative – ἐλυσάμην ("I loosed myself")

Person Singular Plural
1st ἐλυσάμην ἐλυσάμεθα
2nd ἐλύσω ἐλύσασθε
3rd ἐλύσατο ἐλύσαντο

Aorist Passive Indicative – ἐλύθην ("I was loosed")

Person Singular Plural
1st ἐλύθην ἐλύθημεν
2nd ἐλύθης ἐλύθητε
3rd ἐλύθη ἐλύθησαν

Conceptual Categories

  • First vs. Second Aorist: The first aorist employs a σ-marker (e.g., ἔλυσα), while the second aorist uses a modified root without this marker (e.g., ἔλαβον). Despite morphological differences, both forms share the same aspectual function—undefined action.
  • Augment and Aorist Markers: In the indicative mood, aorist verbs begin with an augment (commonly ἐ-) indicating past time. First aorists add -σα- to the stem, whereas second aorists typically alter the stem without a visible marker. Aorist passive forms add -θη- before personal endings.
  • Undefined Aspect in Narrative: The aorist indicative denotes undefined aspect—the action is viewed as a whole without focus on its internal progress or repetition. It often marks single, completed events in narrative discourse, serving as the primary tense for historical narration.
