Present Indicative Verbs

The present indicative in New Testament Greek conveys ongoing, habitual, or repeated action in the present time. This tense-aspect combination is foundational to understanding Greek verb behavior, especially for regular ω-verbs. It uses the present stem with primary endings and lacks an augment, which is reserved for past tense forms.

Key Paradigms

Present Active Indicative – λύω ("I loose")

Person Singular Plural
1st λύω λύομεν
2nd λύεις λύετε
3rd λύει λύουσι(ν)

Present Middle/Passive Indicative – λύομαι ("I am being loosed")

Person Singular Plural
1st λύομαι λυόμεθα
2nd λύῃ / λύει λύεσθε
3rd λύεται λύονται

Conceptual Categories

  • Primary Endings: The present indicative employs primary personal endings. These differ for active and middle/passive forms.
    • Active: -ω, -εις, -ει, -ομεν, -ετε, -ουσι(ν)
    • Middle/Passive: -ομαι, -ῃ/-ει, -εται, -όμεθα, -εσθε, -ονται
  • Present Stem: The verb is built on its unaugmented present stem, which encodes continuous or repeated action. For example, the present stem of λύω is λυ-.
  • No Augment: Unlike past tenses (e.g., imperfect, aorist), the present tense lacks the augment (e.g., ἐ- prefix), indicating non-past time reference.
  • Continuous Aspect: The present indicative expresses action as unfolding in time, often translated with an English progressive ("he is speaking") or iterative/habitual sense ("he speaks regularly").
