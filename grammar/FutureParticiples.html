Future Participles

The future participle in New Testament Greek is a non-finite verbal form that expresses action that is yet to occur, typically with a strong element of purpose or intention. Though it appears relatively infrequently in the New Testament and more often in literary or elevated Greek, its usage remains theologically and syntactically significant. The future participle shares many morphological characteristics with other participial forms but is uniquely marked by its future tense stem and active or middle voice endings.

Key Paradigms

Future Active Participle – λύσων, λύσουσα, λῦσον ("being about to loose")

Conceptual Function

The future participle primarily communicates action that is expected or intended to happen after the main verb’s action. While morphologically built from the future tense stem (e.g., λυσ- from λύω), it retains the adjectival and verbal force of other participles. It agrees in case, number, and gender with the noun it modifies and is declined like a first/second declension adjective.

Its most prominent usage is to express purpose, particularly in construction with verbs of intention or imminence. One of the clearest examples is its combination with μέλλω ("I am about to"), where the participle elaborates the specific action anticipated. For example, μέλλει λυσῶν τὴν πόλιν may be understood as "He is about to loose the city."

In another function, the future participle can have a more predictive force, indicating what someone is expected to do or what will inevitably take place. Though this predictive nuance is less common, it still aligns with the participle’s orientation toward future action.

Overall, the future participle is less frequently attested in the New Testament than its present or aorist counterparts, often appearing in contexts that are either formal, literary, or modeled on classical usage. Understanding its form and function, however, is crucial for the proper interpretation of nuanced theological and narrative developments, especially in Hellenistic Greek texts with elevated or rhetorical style.
