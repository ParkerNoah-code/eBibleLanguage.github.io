Imperatives

The imperative mood in New Testament Greek is the primary verbal form used to express commands, exhortations, and prohibitions. Unlike the indicative, which makes assertions about reality, the imperative directly enjoins the listener or reader to act—or in some cases, to refrain from acting. The imperative exists in the second and third persons only, reflecting the fact that one cannot directly command oneself or make a demand of the first person.

Key Paradigms

2nd Person Imperative – Active Forms of λύω

Aspect Singular Plural
Present (continuous) λῦε λύετε
Aorist (undefined/simple) λῦσον λύσατε
Perfect (completed state) λέλυκε λελύκετε

Conceptual Categories

The imperative mood expresses the speaker’s will or desire for an action to be performed. In the second person, this may appear as a direct command (“λῦσον” – loose!), while in the third person it functions more as a permission or strong exhortation directed toward a third party (“λυσάτω” – let him loose!).

Greek imperatives are not only marked for person and number but also for aspect. The present imperative emphasizes an action that is ongoing, habitual, or general in nature (“λῦε” – keep loosing, continue loosing). In contrast, the aorist imperative presents the action as a whole, undefined event—often perceived as a specific or one-time command (“λῦσον” – loose!). The relatively rare perfect imperative signifies a command to maintain a completed state (“λέλυκε” – continue in the state of having been loosed).

While most attention is given to second-person imperatives in instruction and narrative, third-person imperatives are common in liturgical, legal, or epistolary contexts. They are typically rendered in English with “let him/her/them...” or “may...” constructions. Third-person commands reflect the speaker's desire for action that is not directly executed by the hearer but still within the scope of exhortation or regulation.
