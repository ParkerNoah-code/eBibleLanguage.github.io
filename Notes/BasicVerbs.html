Basic Verbs

Greek verbs communicate tense, voice, mood, person, and number. This section introduces the Present, Imperfect, and Future tenses in the Active Voice and Indicative Mood. These are the foundational verb forms for reading New Testament Greek.

Present Active Indicative

The Present Active Indicative describes ongoing, repeated, or habitual action occurring in the present time. It uses the present stem and primary active endings. This form is often used to state general truths or actions in progress.

Person Singular Plural
1st λύω λύομεν
2nd λύεις λύετε
3rd λύει λύουσι(ν)

Imperfect Active Indicative

The Imperfect Active Indicative describes continuous or repeated action in the past. It uses the present stem, adds an augment (usually ἐ-), and takes secondary active endings. This tense is commonly used to provide background information in narrative texts.

Person Singular Plural
1st ἔλυον ἐλύομεν
2nd ἔλυες ἐλύετε
3rd ἔλυε(ν) ἔλυον

Future Active Indicative

The Future Active Indicative describes a simple action that will occur in the future. It is formed by adding the tense formative σ to the present stem and using primary active endings. This tense expresses predictive future events.

Person Singular Plural
1st λύσω λύσομεν
2nd λύσεις λύσετε
3rd λύσει λύσουσι(ν)

Aspect

Aspect is a core grammatical feature of the Greek verb system, reflecting the speaker’s perspective on the action rather than merely its location in time. While English verbs primarily encode tense (past, present, future), Greek is an aspect-prominent language. This means that the grammatical forms of Greek verbs primarily convey aspect—the type or quality of the action—while time is typically secondary and only explicitly marked in the indicative mood.

Verbal aspect refers to how an action is conceptualized or portrayed by the author: as ongoing, completed, or resulting in a present state. This perspective is not about the objective timing of an action but how the action is subjectively viewed. In Greek, aspect is always present in every verb form, while time reference is explicitly encoded only in the indicative mood and is inferred from context in all non-indicative moods (e.g., subjunctive, imperative, optative, infinitives, and participles).

Aspect Description Common Tenses Typical Translation
Imperfective The action is portrayed as ongoing, continuous, repeated, or in progress without reference to its beginning or end. This aspect emphasizes process, incompletion, or habitual occurrence. Present, Imperfect is teaching, was teaching, keeps teaching
Perfective The action is viewed as a complete whole, with no focus on the internal structure of the event. It presents the action as occurring at a single point in time or as a summary event ("snapshot"). Aorist taught, did teach
Stative (or Resultative) The action is viewed as completed with enduring or resulting relevance to the present or past context. It often combines a sense of completion with a continuing state or consequence. Perfect, Pluperfect has taught, had taught

It is crucial to interpret Greek verbs based on aspect rather than simply time. For example, the Imperfect tense does not merely denote past action—it expresses action that was in progress, incomplete, or iterative in the past. The Aorist, despite often being translated with a past tense in English, does not inherently refer to past time but rather presents the action as a complete, undivided whole. The Perfect tense portrays a completed action with enduring consequences, often interpreted as having current significance.

Understanding Greek aspect is essential for accurate translation and exegesis. Since Greek verbs prioritize how an action is viewed rather than when it occurred, students of the language must analyze both the verb form and the literary context to discern meaning properly.

Verb Morphology

Greek verbs are formed by combining a stem with specific grammatical endings. These components work together to express tense, aspect, voice, mood, person, and number. The core components of a Greek verb form are:

  • Augment – A prefix (usually ἐ-) added in past tenses within the indicative mood
  • Stem – The base or root of the verb that carries the essential meaning
  • Tense Formative – A marker (e.g., σ in the Future) placed between the stem and ending to indicate tense/aspect
  • Connecting Vowel – Often ο/ε used between stem and ending (especially in the present system)
  • Personal Ending – Encodes person (1st, 2nd, 3rd) and number (singular/plural), and sometimes mood and voice

Example – λύσω ("I will release") is broken down as:

  • λυ- (stem)
  • -σ- (future tense formative)
  • -ω (1st person singular, primary active ending)

In the imperfect tense, the verb ἔλυον ("I was releasing") has:

  • ἐ- (augment)
  • λυ- (present stem)
  • -ον (1st person singular, secondary active ending)

Mastery of Greek verb morphology allows learners to recognize and parse verbs correctly in context, especially as they encounter more complex tenses and voices.

Examples

ὁ θεός λέγει τὸν λόγον – “God speaks the word.” Present tense indicates an action occurring now or habitually.

ὁ Ἰησοῦς ἐδίδασκεν τοὺς μαθητάς – “Jesus was teaching the disciples.” The Imperfect shows continuous past action.

πιστεύσομεν εἰς τὸν κύριον – “We will believe in the Lord.” The Future expresses an anticipated action.
