Learning Roadmap

The following roadmap is designed to get readers into the text quickly and to learn the grammar through extensive Scripture reading. I have made a concerted effort to ground grammatical principles in particular texts. One should be able to pick up their Greek New Testament and understand an increasing number of passages as one works their way through these lessons.

Noun Basics is a great place to start. A noun is a person, place, thing, or idea. Greek nouns have cases which inform us of their function in a sentence. They also have grammatical gender: masculine, feminine, or neuter. The article ("the") morphs to match nouns in gender, case, and number (singular or plural). Understand how the paradigmn tables work, reference them when needed for parsing (identifying gender, case, number, and root meaning), and start internalizing them. Basic vocabulary is given, but we will also look at book and passage specific vocabulary.

After learning basic noun functions, several nexts steps are available to us. Third Declension Nouns are the same as basic first and second declension nouns conceptually, but the paradigms are more difficult and irregular. There's no rush to master them early on. Adjectives and Adverbs do not require any further paradigm work. Adjectives, which modify nouns, morph like the nouns they are with, and adverbs, which modify verbs, do not morph at all. Personal and Relative Pronouns can be referenced and will probably be picked up fairly intuitively. Prepositions are used with nouns to express relationships like place or means and can vary in meaning depending on the noun case.

The next major section to start on after learning about basic nouns is basic verbs, beginning with a Verb Overview. It is important to have a strong conceptual background for verbs before beginning to learn the paradigms. One should understand what person, number, voice, mood, and tense refer to. Aspect should especially be focused on. Although the Forms of εἰμί, the verb for "to be", are irregular, they are easy to learn and crucial to master.

Future Indicative Verbs – Verbs expressing simple future action, often using a sigma

Aorist Indicative Verbs – Past tense verbs showing undefined action with two common forms

Liquid Verbs – Verbs with λ, μ, ν, ρ

Infinitives – Verbal nouns expressing action without subject/tense limits

Third Declension Nouns – Nouns with varied stems and complex endings

Perfect and Pluperfect Indicative Verbs – Verbs showing completed action with present or past result

Subjunctive Verbs – Mood used for purpose, potential, or conditional action – Examples

Imperatives – Mood expressing commands or requests – Examples

Present Participles – Verbal adjectives showing present, ongoing action

Aorist Participles – Verbal adjectives showing completed past action

Perfect Participles – Verbal adjectives indicating completed action with result

Future Participles – Verbal adjectives showing intended or future action

Other Pronouns – Demonstrative, Reflexive, Reciprocal, Interrogative, Indefinite

Numbers – Cardinal and ordinal numbers used for counting and order

Optatives – Rare mood expressing wishes or potentiality

μί Verbs – Irregular verbs not using standard ω endings
