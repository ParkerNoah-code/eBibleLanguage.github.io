Learning Roadmap

The following roadmap is designed to get readers into the text quickly and to learn the grammar through extensive Scripture reading. I have made a concerted effort to ground grammatical principles in particular texts. One should be able to pick up their Greek New Testament and understand an increasing number of passages as one works their way through these lessons.

Noun Basics is a great place to start. A noun is a person, place, thing, or idea. Greek nouns have cases which inform us of their function in a sentence. They also have grammatical gender: masculine, feminine, or neuter. The article ("the") morphs to match nouns in gender, case, and number (singular or plural). You should understand how the paradigm tables work. Start memorizing the first and second declension noun endings so that you can begin parsing without constantly checking the chart.

Exercise: I recommend starting with Mark 1.

After learning basic noun functions, several intermediate steps are available to us. Third Declension Nouns are the same as basic first and second declension nouns conceptually, but the paradigms are more difficult and irregular. There's no rush to master them early on. Adjectives and Adverbs do not require any further paradigm work. Adjectives, which modify nouns, morph like the nouns they are with, and adverbs, which modify verbs, do not morph at all. Personal and Relative Pronouns can be referenced and will probably be picked up fairly intuitively. Prepositions are used with nouns to express relationships like place or means and can vary in meaning depending on the case of the noun they are with.

The next major topic after learning about basic nouns is indicative verbs, beginning with a Verb Overview. We will start developing a strong conceptual understanding of verbs before going over paradigms. One should understand what person, number, voice, mood, tense, and aspect mean. Aspect should especially be focused on. After the overview, the first paradigms we will learn are the Forms of εἰμί (the verb for "to be"). Even though they are irregular, they are easy to learn and absolutely crucial in Koine Greek.

The first regular verb tense paradigms to learn are the Present Indicative Verbs and Imperfect Indicative Verbs. They both fall under the same aspect indicating ongoing or repeated action but are used for the present and past respectively. Related to this, Contract Verbs describe the way vowel stems combine with endings which often happens with morphology in these tenses. After this, we can learn about Future Indicative Verbs which are basically the same as present verbs but with a sigma added to the stem.

After studying the first three tenses, we can move on to the Aorist Indicative Verbs. They have two common forms, one with a sigma and one without. Liquid Verbs describe the morphology for the verbs ending with λ, μ, ν, ρ. This equips us to learn about Infinitives which are verbal nouns. We can also learn the last two major verb tenses with Perfect and Pluperfect Indicative Verbs.

To finish the major verb paradigms, we can move on to the non-indicative moods with Subjunctives, Imperatives, and Optatives. Lastly for verbs, we have μί Verbs which are a special class of irregular verbs that do not use the standard ω endings. They are not used often, but they are important. However, you could choose to come back to them at the end. Other Pronouns and Numbers can be learned at any time.

The last major topic is participles. They are verbal adjectives that can be used in many ways. We will learn the four main types: Present Participles, Aorist Participles, Perfect Participles, and Future Participles. They can be used to express action in relation to the main verb of a clause.
