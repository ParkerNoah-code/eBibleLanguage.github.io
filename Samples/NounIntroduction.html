Noun Introduction

Noun Basics

"Declension" refers to a pattern. There are 3 primary noun declensions. However, the first and second declensions are the easiest to learn and occur the most often so that's what we will examine in this lesson.

To learn the vocabulary for this section, each Greek noun will be given a gloss (a basic translation). The gloss is always based on the nominative singular form. The case, either nominative, genitive, dative, or accusative, as well as the number, either singular or plural, is reflected by changes at the end of the noun.

Note, there is also a vocative case, but it will be very easy to spot and understand when it comes up later on. It is also not used very often.

Right now, it is sufficient to identify the case based on the ending of the word and to have a simple idea of how the cases function. We will nuance the case functions later.

First declension nouns are feminine primarily, ending in either -η or -α. The plural forms are the same for both of these endings, and the singular forms of -η or -α ending words are only altered very slightly.

Second declension nouns are primarily masculine -ος ending or neuter -ον ending words. There are only a few small differences between these paradigms also.

Vocabulary

There are three articles, one for each gender, which also decline (change) according to case and number to match the noun they are with. The article is included in our vocabulary cards for us to learn the grammatical gender of the noun which is crucial for understanding which paradigm applies to the noun.

The definite article genders are as follows: ἡ is feminine, ὁ is masculine, and τὸ is neuter.
